85% of News Outlets Strip Image Metadata, Harming Creators

Dainis W. Michel April 8, 2025 3 min read

A devastating study by Digital Photography Review has confirmed what photographers have long suspected: 85% of online news outlets strip vital metadata from images. This systematic erasure doesn't just remove technical information—it disconnects creators from their work entirely.

"Without metadata, images become orphaned works—creators lose not just attribution, but their ability to assert ownership, receive payment, and protect their copyright."

— International Press Telecommunications Council

Attribution Extinction by Technical Design

This isn't a minor technical oversight—it's a fundamental design flaw affecting the entire visual media ecosystem. When image metadata is removed, photographers lose:

  • Attribution and recognition for their work
  • Contact information for potential clients
  • Licensing details specifying usage rights
  • Copyright information necessary for enforcing rights
  • Ability to track usage across platforms

Even more concerning, this happens at a systemic level. Nearly every major CMS and image optimization system automatically strips metadata during processing, meaning publishers often remove vital creator information even when they don't intend to.

A Legal and Ethical Imperative

Recent legal precedents, including the landmark Philpot v. WOS case, have established that removing image metadata constitutes a violation of copyright management information under the DMCA. Media organizations now face increased legal exposure, making DA1's attribution solutions not just technically sound but legally prudent.

Beyond legal concerns, there's an ethical imperative: creators deserve recognition and compensation for their work. When 85% of media outlets inadvertently sever that connection, we need solutions that restore it by design.
