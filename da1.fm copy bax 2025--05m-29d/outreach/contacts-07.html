Music

Björn Ulvaeus

Role: Songwriter, Musician (ABBA), President of CISAC, Founder of Credits Due Initiative

Twitter: N/A
"The vast amount of new songs released on a daily basis, many with very little metadata and missing linked identifiers causes enormous problems for the music industry. [This is] the reason why so much royalty money each year is unidentified and not paid."
Source: Credits Due Initiative launch, 2021, https://www.nme.com/news/music/abbas-bjorn-ulvaeus-launches-credits-due-campaign-to-fix-music-royalty-issue-3052041
"It's very simple, music recordings must credit all involved and thus ensure that the right people get paid. People ask me why this isn't the case already and I don't know what to say. Today, in 2021, there's really no excuse. If we achieve Credits Due it's a win-win for the whole music industry."
Source: Variety, 2021, https://variety.com/2021/music/news/abba-bjorn-ulveus-credits-due-songwriters-1235070129/

Björn Ulvaeus, best known as a member of ABBA, launched the Credits Due initiative in collaboration with The Ivors Academy and The Music Rights Awareness Foundation (which he co-founded). The initiative, unveiled at the Ivor Novello awards in September 2021, aims to ensure that complete and accurate song metadata is attached to all recordings at the point of creation. Ulvaeus has highlighted that approximately £500 million ($625 million) in royalties is unallocated or misallocated each year worldwide due to missing and incomplete metadata. The Credits Due initiative proposes that all new recordings and songs include five key metadata elements: song titles (including alternative titles), musical work identifiers, creator identifiers and role codes, recording identifiers, and details of all contributors. The problem is growing more acute with 60,000 new tracks added to Spotify daily. Ulvaeus serves as the President of CISAC (International Confederation of Societies of Authors and Composers) and co-founder of The Music Rights Awareness Foundation (MRAF).

Jim Griffin

Role: Managing Director, OneHouse LLC; Digital Rights Expert

Twitter: N/A
"Metadata is the biggest little problem plaguing the music industry."
Source: Widely attributed in industry discussions, https://entertainment.slashdot.org/story/19/05/30/2018200/metadata-is-the-biggest-little-problem-plaguing-the-music-industry

Jim Griffin is the Managing Director of OneHouse LLC, an organization dedicated to the future of music and entertainment delivery. A forward-thinking technologist and industry pioneer, Griffin has been at the forefront of digital music issues for decades. He started and ran the technology department at Geffen Records for five years, where in June 1994, he led a team that distributed the first full-length commercial song online. Griffin teaches music copyright law and focuses on the digital delivery of art, especially music and its monetization. He works particularly on the implementation of the Music Modernization Act and its global impact. Griffin has been directly involved with industry metadata initiatives, participating in the Information Infrastructure panel focused on the executive business case for metadata standards. His expertise in digital rights and metadata management has made him a respected voice on addressing the critical metadata challenges facing the music industry today. Griffin has been described as understanding "that the most important, complex, and broken part of the music industry" is metadata.

Music

Alison Moses

Role: Music Supervisor

Twitter: N/A
"A lot of people think I want WAV files, but they don't hold any metadata, so they're kind of useless."
Source: Cited in industry discussions on metadata, https://community.metabrainz.org/t/metadata-is-the-biggest-little-problem-plaguing-the-music-industry/430027

Alison Moses is a music supervisor who has highlighted a critical issue with WAV files in the music industry - their inability to hold persistent metadata. This technical limitation creates significant workflow challenges for music supervisors, who need accurate and complete metadata to properly organize, search, and license music for film, television, and other media projects. Moses's frustration is "often shared by music journalists, music supervisors and really anyone who's sent music in bulk, as improper or missing metadata can seriously impact their ability to engage with a piece of music—even when they like what they hear." The issue she identifies is particularly problematic because WAV files are considered the industry standard for high-quality audio, yet they're among "the most egregious offenders when it comes to missing information." This technical disconnect between audio quality and metadata preservation creates a fundamental challenge in the music licensing workflow.
