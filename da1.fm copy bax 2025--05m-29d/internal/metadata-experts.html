Metadata Experts in Visual Media

A comprehensive list of professionals who have discussed metadata issues specifically in photography, film, visual arts, and publishing. These individuals have made public statements about metadata problems and advocated for better practices.

David Riecks

Co-Lead, IPTC Photo Metadata Working Group & Professional Photographer

"Many, many digital photo creators don't even know that metadata can be saved within an image file."

For professional photographers, embedded metadata is "an easy way to let potential publishers know they took the photo and how to contact them. Storing this information inside the image can't prevent others from misusing the information but it can help others know more about the image: who is pictured in a photo, what they are doing (and maybe why) as well as where and when it was taken. However, all of those benefits are lost if this metadata doesn't 'stick' to the image as it travels from one computer to another and onto the web."

"Metadata acts as contextual agent, documenting the 'who, what, where, when, why, and how', and gives an image identity and authenticity. Once data about the image is removed much of its meaning is lost. This can lead to unfortunate consequences. Under an act recently passed in the UK these orphaned photos could be used by for-profit companies without the consent of the copyright holder(s)."

David Riecks (rhymes with "clicks") is a professional photographer, digital-image technologist, and metadata evangelist who has been heavily involved in promoting and developing photo metadata standards. He chaired the Digital Photography Standards & Practices committees of the American Society of Media Photographers (ASMP) and was a founding member of the Universal Photographic Digital Imaging Guidelines coalition.

Michael Steidl

Former Managing Director, IPTC (International Press Telecommunications Council)

"There are 'lots of organizations creating schemas, and many of their properties are quite similar in terms of semantics.' The problem: there are reasonable schemas for different areas of creative work in a given area, but 'looking across boundaries it is hard to bring them together.'"

"Much time and money is spent to protect metadata in in-house systems... Therefore it is a business requirement to protect the descriptive data and rights information from getting lost."

"There are many important reasons to embed and preserve metadata – to protect copyrights, ensure proper licensing, track image use, smooth workflow, and make them searchable on- or offline. If users provide captions, dates, a copyright notice and the creator within their images, that data shouldn't be removed when sharing them on social media websites without their knowledge."

"The quality assurance of these sites might not be aware that their software strips metadata inadvertently."

Michael Steidl served as Managing Director of IPTC for 15 years. He was responsible for the wide-ranging work of the IPTC, an organization deeply involved in the development and use of metadata standards for 35 years. Under his leadership, IPTC conducted studies on metadata preservation across social media platforms and advocated for better metadata preservation practices.

Sarah Saunders

Digital Asset Management Consultant & Member of IPTC Photo Metadata Working Group

"As a photographer, I care deeply about creator copyright, but it goes further than that."

"Many museums have images on their sites which can be control-click downloaded but there is no attribution or informative metadata embedded."

"It is perfectly possible [to embed metadata], and the museums who are pioneering making their images available to the public, like Rijks Museum, National Gallery of Art Washington and British Museum Picture Library, do have embedded metadata."

Sarah Saunders runs consultancy Electric Lane, working on image management and archiving projects. She is the author of the IPTC/CEPIC Metadata Handbook and has been a significant contributor to developing the IPTC Cultural Heritage Panel, designed to address the needs of museums, art foundations, libraries, and archive organizations.

LinkedIn: @electriclane

Jeff Sedlik

President & CEO, PLUS Coalition (Picture Licensing Universal System)

Jeff Sedlik is a photographer, director, educator, publisher, and consultant who serves as President of the PLUS Coalition. He is recognized as a leading expert on image licensing, copyright, and the business of photography. As a past National President of the APA (Advertising Photographers of America), he has been extensively involved in advocating for photographers' rights.

The PLUS Coalition, under Sedlik's leadership, has worked to address the "complete lack of respect for photographer credits and copyrights" where "metadata is regularly stripped in its entirety." This forces photographers who care about their copyrights and ownership to place their metadata offline, separate from their photos.

General Counsel and Head of National Content and Education, ASMP

Thomas Maddrey serves as the ASMP (American Society of Media Photographers) General Counsel and has a unique background as both a professional commercial photographer and an attorney. Zoe Keating

Cellist, Composer & Music Technology Advocate

"I think because I am outside the music industry, much of the way it operates seems absurd to me, so I talk about it."

On her vision for a music metadata solution: A "distributed ledger of music metadata" that "could keep track not only who created what, but who else was materially involved, from the producer, to the side musicians, to the people who promote it, to the samples taken from another song."

She imagines "a ledger of all that information and an ecosystem of killer apps to visualize usage and relationships" and "a music exchange where the real value of a song could be calculated on the fly."

Zoë Keating is a Canadian-American cellist and composer who has become a prominent voice advocating for better metadata practices in the music industry. She is one of the few artists to regularly publish her revenue from streaming services to help others understand the economics of digital distribution. Her advocacy for transparency extends to metadata issues that affect proper attribution and compensation.

Rainer Steußloff

Berlin-based Photographer & Legal Pioneer

(Successfully sued Facebook for stripping metadata from images, arguing that this practice "violates German Copyright law, specifically, section 95c of the Copyright Act," which states that "the rights holder's information may not be removed or altered.")

Rainer Steußloff is a Berlin-based photographer who made legal history by successfully suing Facebook for stripping metadata from images. The case established an important precedent in German courts, making it illegal for Facebook to strip metadata from images uploaded by German photographers. This ruling opened the door for similar suits in other countries and brought international attention to the issue of metadata preservation in social media.

James Tauber

Digital Humanities Scholar & Software Developer

"We would never agree to insert a block citation in an analysis if we did not know where it came from and say 'Yes, but we can see what is written here in this block citation'. Yet if we do not know who has written it, for what purpose, where it has been published, where it comes from and who it was supposed to target it is quite useless as a historical source."

"Yet this is how images are often used, like some general homeless block citations which are supposed to be able to speak by themselves."

James Tauber has worked on digital humanities projects and has highlighted the problems with using digital images without proper metadata. His powerful analogy comparing images without metadata to uncited block quotes illustrates how critical metadata is for establishing the provenance and context of visual materials.
