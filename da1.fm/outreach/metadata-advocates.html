Metadata Advocates in Creative Industries

The following individuals have been vocal about metadata challenges across creative sectors including music, photography, film, and publishing. Their insights highlight the widespread impact of metadata problems and the need for better solutions.

David Lowery

Songwriter, Musician (Camper Van Beethoven, Cracker), Professor

Twitter: @davidclowery
"Well, the first thing they have to understand is that there's not a single songwriter or musician that I know that is against streaming services. It's like saying the coal miners when they strike against the coal mines are against coal mines. No, they're striking for fair pay. We're in a great industrial transition again, and usually when that happens, the content workers get the short end of the stick until this stuff gets sorted out."
Source: The Washington Post, 2017
"My Song Got Played on Pandora 1 Million Times and All I Got Was $16.89"
Source: Essay title that went viral, 2012

David Lowery has been a vocal advocate for musicians' rights in the digital age. As a professor of economics and finance of the music business at the University of Georgia, he has issued influential essays on musicians' rights and has taken legal action against streaming services for inadequate royalty payments. In 2015, he filed a class-action lawsuit against Spotify, seeking at least $150 million in damages for unpaid mechanical royalties due to inadequate metadata systems. The lawsuit highlighted the disconnect between publishing/songwriting metadata and recording metadata that prevented proper compensation.

Björn Ulvaeus

Songwriter, Musician (ABBA), President of CISAC

Twitter: N/A
"The vast amount of new songs released on a daily basis, many with very little metadata and missing linked identifiers causes enormous problems for the music industry. [This is] the reason why so much royalty money each year is unidentified and not paid."
Source: Credits Due Initiative launch, 2021
"If streaming rates had been the same when I started my career, I would have become a civil engineer because I was going to be educated at university."
Source: Music Week interview, 2021

Björn Ulvaeus launched the "Credits Due" initiative in September 2021 to ensure that "complete and accurate song metadata is attached to all recordings at the point of creation." As CISAC president and co-founder of The Music Rights Awareness Foundation (MRAF), he has highlighted that approximately £500 million (about $625 million) in royalties goes unallocated or is misallocated each year due to missing and incomplete metadata. He has worked with Max Martin and Niclas Molinder to develop "Session Studio," a technology that automatically captures song metadata during the creation process.

Maria Schneider

Jazz Composer, Bandleader, Grammy Award Winner

Twitter: @mschneider_jazz
"If 'digital music' is ever going to provide us a reasonable economic future, it will be based on metadata. Thus, I urge every musician and performer to demand that their metadata be kept in-tact, and not be destroyed."
Source: Congressional testimony, 2020
"YouTube strips that Copyright Information right off of our files [like] a power chipper on steroids, removing metadata from literally billions of files."
Source: DMCA hearing testimony, 2020

Maria Schneider is a Grammy-winning composer who has been at the forefront of advocacy for musicians' rights, especially regarding metadata and digital platforms. In July 2020, she filed a class-action lawsuit against YouTube, arguing that smaller copyright holders were denied access to Content ID tools that would help protect their work. She has testified before Congress on DMCA shortcomings, emphasizing the need for platforms to maintain metadata integrity and implement better fingerprinting technology. Her advocacy influenced her double-album "Data Lords" (2020), which addressed themes of digital control and exploitation.

T Bone Burnett

Record Producer, Musician, Songwriter

Twitter: N/A
"For someone who's been doing this for 40 years and spent so much time trying to get the sound right, the way we want it, to have it then leave our hands and have maybe 10 people with no connection to the work making important decisions about how it sounds is unacceptable. The time is right for the artist to take control of the whole process, from production to manufacturing to delivery."
Source: Interview about his CODE technology
"We can put colors in the coating, and in those colors, you can store digital information—a CD, DVD, Blu Ray, holograms, metadata, album covers, interviews, anything you want."
Source: Mix Magazine, discussing Ionic Originals format, 2022

T Bone Burnett has been an advocate for audio quality and proper artist control throughout his career. He developed the CODE format in the early 2000s to offer higher-quality audio that preserved the artist's intent. More recently, he unveiled the Ionic Original format, which combines analog sound quality with the ability to embed digital metadata. His advocacy stems from concerns about how digital distribution has stripped artists of control over how their work is presented and how it sounds, emphasizing that records are important cultural documents that should maintain their integrity.

Don Henley

Musician, Songwriter (Eagles), Co-founder of Recording Artists' Coalition

Twitter: N/A
"Due to the antiquated procedures dictated by the DMCA, internet services with clear oversight and control of content posted on their websites are continuing to monetize and collect advertising revenue on videos containing music, even after that music has been flagged by the music creator as infringing."
Source: Congressional testimony, June 2, 2020
"The internet is slowly but surely killing the idea of copyright."
Source: Digital Music News, 2015

Don Henley has been a vocal advocate for musicians' rights throughout his career. In 2000, he co-founded the Recording Artists' Coalition to protect musicians from unfair industry practices. He has testified before Congress multiple times regarding copyright issues, including advocating for updates to the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA). Henley has recommended that Congress amend the DMCA to hold internet service providers and websites like YouTube accountable for hosting infringing content, comparing their responsibility to that of physical distributors and retailers who have legal liability for selling bootleg music.

Jeff Price

Founder of TuneCore, Co-founder of Audiam, CEO of Word Collections

Twitter: @jpriceofmusic
"When you paint a picture of hopelessness for artists, suggest they are failures and attempt to discourage them based on a nonsensical math equation and incomplete data, then you've lashed out at the wrong target. They deserve the truth."
Source: Response to criticism of artists earning money, 2011

Jeff Price has been at the forefront of digital music distribution and metadata management for decades. As the founder of TuneCore, he revolutionized how independent artists could distribute their music digitally without giving up rights or revenue. Later, as the founder of Audiam, he focused specifically on "locating metadata for every sound recording of a specific composition, recovering due mechanical rights revenue for songwriters, composers and music publishers." Price has worked to create more transparent royalty systems with easy access for artists, and has been vocal about the importance of proper metadata for ensuring fair compensation in the digital music ecosystem.

David Israelite

President and CEO of National Music Publishers' Association (NMPA)

Twitter: @DavidIsraelite
"Anyone who enters this space is going to get sued because it's practically impossible to license content properly. In the old days, when music was physically sold, the record labels would be the ones seeking the mechanical license. They'd come to us and say 'We need these 10 songs.' Today, I have Amazon in my office saying 'How do we license 40 million songs tomorrow?'"
Source: Congressional testimony on music licensing
"[We need] a transparent database paid for 100% by the digital companies that use it but managed by creative community."
Source: Interview on metadata solutions

David Israelite has led the NMPA since 2005, representing American music publishers and songwriters. He has characterized the music licensing system as "broken" due to metadata problems that make proper licensing nearly impossible at scale. Under his leadership, the NMPA has achieved significant victories for publishers, including landmark settlements with streaming platforms and playing a key role in the passage of the Music Modernization Act, which created the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC) to address metadata and licensing challenges. Israelite has consistently argued that publishing rights are often treated as a secondary concern in the music industry despite being the primary right.

Jim Griffin

Media Technologist, VP of Digital Rights at Pex, Former Warner Music Group Executive

LinkedIn: @onehouse
"I've been following Pex closely for years and have watched it grow from just a vision to its current state as the best-in-class identification technology. I'm enthralled to be part of the company that will bring long-awaited transparency and real-time copyright licensing to social media and other user generated content."
Source: Statement upon joining Pex

Jim Griffin is a media technologist who teaches music copyright law with a focus on digital delivery and monetization of creative works. His experience includes roles as CTO of Geffen Records, CEO of Cherry Lane Digital, and President of Network Licensing at Warner Music Group. Griffin has published on music rights, including "Rights Unenumerated, Rights Disrespected: The Need for a Global Music Registry." He co-founded the influential Pho List, which began as a weekly meeting where industry professionals discussed digital transformation of the music industry. Currently as VP of Digital Rights at Pex, he works on technologies that can identify content and enable proper licensing and attribution.

Ani DiFranco

Singer-Songwriter, Founder of Righteous Babe Records

Twitter: @AnDiFranco
"The tools of capitalism that I disdain are some of the very things that allow me to be an independent artist."
Source: Interview on industry independence

While specific quotes about metadata were not found, Ani DiFranco has been a pioneer of musical independence, founding her own record label at age 18 to maintain control over her music and artistic integrity. Her DIY approach has been a defining characteristic of her career, allowing her complete control over her recordings, distribution, and rights management. Through Righteous Babe Records, she has maintained ownership of her master recordings and publishing rights, serving as a model for how artists can retain control of their work and associated metadata in a challenging industry landscape.

Debbie Harry

Singer-Songwriter, Blondie

Twitter: @BlondieOfficial
"As a writer and performer, I want to get paid for what I do."
Source: Multiple interviews
"Even funnier was when 'Heart Of Glass' was at number one, we were on suspension, which means that we hadn't fulfilled the obligations of our contract. So we had a number one record and we were also on suspension. The whole thing of it was mad, it was really mad."
Source: Red Bull Music Academy lecture

Debbie Harry has taken a practical approach to music rights management throughout her long career. In 2020, she and Chris Stein sold their future royalties to the Hipgnosis Songs Fund, giving the investment company ownership of income from 197 Blondie songs. They described it as "working with a progressive company," indicating their belief in new approaches to rights management. Harry has consistently emphasized the importance of fair compensation for creative work and has spoken about the contradictions and challenges of the music business model from the perspective of a working musician.

Tom Waits

Musician, Songwriter, Actor

Twitter: N/A
"When a guy is singing to me about toilet paper - you may need the money but, I mean, rob a 7-11! Do something with dignity and save us all the trouble of peeing on your grave."
Source: 1987 interview on advertising offers
"If you subscribe to your personal mythology, to the point where you do your own work, and then somebody puts decals over it, it no longer carries the same weight."
Source: Interview on commercial use of music

Tom Waits has been a fierce defender of artists' rights, particularly regarding the unauthorized commercial use of their work. He has successfully sued companies that have attempted to use soundalikes of his distinctive voice, winning a $2.6 million verdict against Frito-Lay. Waits has consistently refused to allow his music to be used in advertising, stating that "artists who take money for ads poison and pervert their songs." His legal battles have helped establish precedents for protecting artists' distinctive styles and voices, and he has maintained that "his songs are still not for sale at any price and neither is his voice," demonstrating his commitment to controlling how his creative work is used.
