Hollywood's Metadata Mess Costs Studios Billions

Dainis W. Michel April 9, 2025 3 min read

When you think of Hollywood studios, you imagine state-of-the-art technology and seamless digital workflows. Yet according to a revealing Variety report, even these entertainment giants are mired in a metadata crisis that delays digital content distribution by months and costs the industry billions in unrealized revenue.

"The average movie today contains hundreds of metadata fields, all of which must be properly populated across dozens of platforms—and the industry lacks any true standardization."

— MovieLabs, as reported by Variety

Hollywood's Digital Bottleneck

When one of the world's most technologically advanced industries struggles with metadata consistency, it reveals the depth of the problem. The current challenges include:

  • Distribution delays of up to 12 weeks due to metadata reconciliation
  • Format fragmentation across dozens of streaming and VOD platforms
  • Manual rework costing thousands of labor hours per title
  • Metadata inconsistencies that cause discovery and monetization failures
  • Rights management chaos that prevents proper revenue attribution

These aren't issues affecting indie productions alone—they plague every major studio, from Disney to Warner Bros., Universal to Sony. When giants with vast technical resources struggle this profoundly, it signals a fundamental structural problem rather than an implementation failure.

Beyond Technical Solutions

The industry has attempted technical fixes like the Entertainment Identifier Registry (EIDR) and various metadata standards. Yet these haven't solved the core problem because they address symptoms rather than the underlying structural issue: metadata that exists separately from content will always fragment across systems.

DA1's innovation lies in making attribution and rights information an inherent, inseparable part of the content itself—not just an attached label that can be lost or corrupted through the distribution process.

This approach aligns perfectly with MovieLabs' 2030 Vision for media creation, which calls for fundamental rethinking of how content and its associated information move through the ecosystem.
